# Criminal Law Domain Knowledge (KM500)

## Overview
Criminal law deals with offences against the state, prosecution of alleged offenders, criminal defences, sentencing, and appeals. This domain encompasses Commonwealth and State/Territory criminal legislation, including the Criminal Code Act 1995 (Cth) and various State criminal codes and crimes acts.

## 2. Classification Structure

### Offence Categories

#### Indictable Offences
**Definition:** Serious criminal offences that may be tried before a judge and jury.

**Characteristics:**
- Right to trial by jury (except where waived)
- Tried in District/County or Supreme Court
- Maximum penalties typically exceed 2 years imprisonment
- More formal procedural requirements
- Committal proceedings in Magistrates Court

**Examples:**
- Murder and manslaughter
- Serious sexual offences (rape, sexual assault)
- Robbery (especially armed robbery)
- Serious fraud offences
- Drug trafficking/commercial supply
- Aggravated burglary
- Serious assault causing grievous bodily harm

**Sub-categories:**
- **Serious indictable offences** (NSW): Maximum penalty of 5+ years imprisonment
- **Major indictable offences** (SA): Triable in Supreme Court
- **Indictable offences (first schedule)** (Vic): Most serious category

#### Summary Offences
**Definition:** Minor criminal offences dealt with summarily (without jury) in Magistrates Court.

**Characteristics:**
- Heard by magistrate alone (no jury)
- Less formal procedures
- Lower maximum penalties (typically under 2 years)
- Quicker resolution
- No committal hearing

**Examples:**
- Common assault (no injury)
- Low-level theft/shoplifting
- Minor criminal damage
- Driving offences (most)
- Offensive language/behaviour
- Public intoxication
- Minor drug possession
- Trespass
- Breach of intervention order

**Relevant Legislation:**
- Summary Offences Act 1988 (NSW)
- Summary Offences Act 1966 (Vic)
- Summary Offences Act 1953 (SA)
- Police Offences Act 1935 (Tas)
- Summary Offences Act 1923 (ACT)
- Summary Offences Act 1923 (NT)

#### Indictable Offences Treated Summarily
**Definition:** Indictable offences that may be dealt with summarily in the Magistrates Court.

**Requirements:**
- Accused must consent (in most jurisdictions)
- Court must consider appropriate venue
- Maximum penalty in summary hearing is reduced

**Common Examples:**
- Theft under specified value (e.g., $5,000)
- Fraud under specified value
- Less serious assault charges
- Burglary (non-aggravated)
- Receiving stolen property
- Certain drug offences

**Jurisdictional Variations:**
- **NSW:** Criminal Procedure Act 1986, Table 1 and Table 2
- **Victoria:** Criminal Procedure Act 2009, Schedule 2
- **Queensland:** Criminal Code s 552 series
- **Western Australia:** Criminal Procedure Act 2004
- **South Australia:** Summary Procedure Act 1921
- **Tasmania:** Justices Act 1959

**Advantages:**
- Quicker resolution
- Lower costs
- Less formal proceedings
- Reduced maximum penalties

**Disadvantages:**
- No right to jury trial in summary hearing
- Limited sentencing options
- May not be appropriate for serious cases

### Liability Categories

#### Strict Liability Offences
**Definition:** Offences where proof of fault element (mens rea) is not required for one or more physical elements.

**Key Features:**
- Prosecution need not prove intention, knowledge or recklessness
- Defence of honest and reasonable mistake of fact available
- Generally regulatory offences

**Commonwealth Law:**
- Criminal Code s 6.1
- Defence of mistake of fact under s 9.2

**Examples:**
- Many traffic offences
- Environmental offences
- Food safety offences
- Workplace health and safety offences
- Liquor licensing offences
- Some drug possession offences

**Policy Rationale:**
- Public welfare protection
- Administrative efficiency
- Encourages compliance
- Regulatory in nature

#### Absolute Liability Offences
**Definition:** Offences where no fault element is required and no defences available except involuntariness.

**Key Features:**
- No mens rea required
- No defence of mistake of fact
- Only defence: physical element not voluntary
- Rare and strictly construed

**Commonwealth Law:**
- Criminal Code s 6.2
- Must be expressly specified in legislation

**Examples:**
- Some parking offences
- Some statutory declaration offences
- Rare regulatory offences

**Constitutional Issues:**
- Must be clearly indicated in legislation
- Subject to constitutional challenges
- Limited to minor regulatory matters

#### Fault-Based Offences
**Definition:** Traditional offences requiring proof of mens rea (guilty mind).

**Mens Rea Categories:**
- **Intention:** Purpose or aim to bring about result
- **Knowledge:** Awareness of circumstances or consequences
- **Recklessness:** Awareness of substantial risk, unjustifiable to take risk
- **Negligence:** Failure to exercise reasonable care, substantial risk

## 3. Key Legal Concepts & Keywords

### Mental Elements (Mens Rea)

#### Intention
**Definition:** The purpose or aim to bring about a specific result or circumstance.

**Key Concepts:**
- Direct intention: Desire to bring about outcome
- Oblique intention: Knowledge that outcome is virtually certain
- Specific intent offences: Require proof of particular mental state

**Relevant Sections:**
- Criminal Code (Cth) s 5.2: Intention

**Common Terminology:**

- "Deliberately"

**Case Law Keywords:**
- *R v Moloney* [1985]: Oblique intention test
- *R v Woollin* [1998]: Virtual certainty test
- *He Kaw Teh v The Queen* (1985): Intention in drug importation

#### Knowledge
**Definition:** Awareness of facts, circumstances, or consequences.

**Key Concepts:**
- Actual knowledge: Person actually knows
- Constructive knowledge: Person ought to know (higher threshold)
- Wilful blindness: Deliberately avoiding knowledge

**Relevant Sections:**
- Criminal Code (Cth) s 5.3: Knowledge

**Common Terminology:**
- "Knowing that"
- "Knowing or believing"
- "Aware that"
- "Having reason to believe"
- "Wilfully blind"

**Case Law Keywords:**
- *Giorgianni v The Queen* (1985): Knowledge of essential facts
- *He Kaw Teh v The Queen* (1985): Wilful blindness
- *Crabbe v The Queen* (1985): Actual knowledge vs belief

#### Recklessness
**Definition:** Awareness of substantial risk that circumstance exists or will result, and unjustifiable to take that risk.

**Key Concepts:**
- Subjective awareness of risk
- Risk must be substantial and unjustifiable
- Distinguishable from mere carelessness
- Higher than negligence, lower than intention

**Relevant Sections:**
- Criminal Code (Cth) s 5.4: Recklessness
- Crimes Act 1900 (NSW) s 18: Murder—reckless indifference

**Common Terminology:**
- "Recklessly"

**Case Law Keywords:**
- *Crabbe v The Queen* (1985): Subjective recklessness
- *R v Cunningham* [1957]: Malicious—means reckless
- *Aubrey v The Queen* (2017): Recklessness in murder

#### Negligence
**Definition:** Conduct falling below the standard of care that a reasonable person would exercise in the circumstances.

**Key Concepts:**
- Objective test: Reasonable person standard
- Degree varies: Simple negligence vs gross negligence
- Criminal negligence: Great falling short of standard
- Distinguished from civil negligence (lower threshold)

**Relevant Sections:**
- Criminal Code (Cth) s 5.5: Negligence
- Criminal Code (Qld) s 289: Negligence

**Common Terminology:**
- "Negligently"

- *R v Lavender* (2005): Gross negligence manslaughter
- *R v Taktak* (1988): Negligent driving causing death

### Physical Elements (Actus Reus)

#### Conduct
**Definition:** The physical act or omission that constitutes the offence.

**Key Concepts:**
- Voluntary conduct: Must be willed by accused
- Involuntary conduct: No criminal liability
- Omissions: Failure to act where duty exists

**Relevant Sections:**
- Criminal Code (Cth) s 4.1: Physical elements
- Criminal Code (Cth) s 11.2: Voluntariness

**Common Terminology:**
- "Does an act"
- "Engages in conduct"

- Involuntary intoxication

**Case Law Keywords:**

- *R v Falconer* (1990): Automatism
- *Jiminez v The Queen* (1992): Voluntariness

#### Circumstance
**Definition:** The facts or state of affairs that must exist for conduct to constitute an offence.

**Examples:**
- Age of victim (e.g., child under 16)
- Status of person (e.g., police officer)
- Relationship (e.g., position of authority)
- Consent (or lack thereof)
- Ownership of property
- Location (e.g., dwelling house)

**Common Terminology:**
- "In circumstances where"
- "Where the person is"
- "If the victim is"
- "Without consent"
- "In a dwelling"

#### Result
**Definition:** The consequence that must follow from the conduct for the offence to be complete.

**Key Concepts:**
- Death (homicide offences)
- Injury or harm (assault offences)
- Property loss (theft, damage)
- Deception (fraud offences)

**Common Terminology:**

### Causation

**Intervening Acts:**

### Defences

#### Self-Defence

#### Duress
**Definition:** Compulsion by threats of immediate death or serious harm to commit an offence.

**Elements:**

3. No reasonable means of escape

**Relevant Sections:**

**Key Concepts:**

#### Necessity
**Definition:** Conduct necessary to prevent serious harm, where harm avoided greater than harm caused.

**Elements:**
1. Sudden or extraordinary emergency
2. Reasonable belief conduct necessary
3. Conduct proportionate response
4. No other reasonable way to deal with emergency

**Relevant Sections:**
- Criminal Code (Cth) s 10.3: Sudden or extraordinary emergency
- Crimes Act 1958 (Vic) s 322O: Sudden or extraordinary emergency

#### Mental Impairment (Insanity)
**Definition:** Person not criminally responsible if mental impairment means they did not know nature and quality of conduct, or did not know conduct was wrong.

1. Defect of reason
2. Caused by disease of the mind

**Relevant Sections:**

**Key Concepts:**

**Common Terminology:**

#### Provocation
**Definition:** Loss of self-control caused by conduct of deceased, reducing murder to manslaughter.

**Status:**
- Abolished in many jurisdictions (Vic 2005, WA 2008, Tas 2003)
- Retained in modified form in others (NSW, Qld, SA, NT)

**Elements (where retained):**
1. Wrongful act or insult by victim
2. Caused loss of self-control
3. Ordinary person would have lost self-control
4. Act done in heat of passion, not from revenge

**Relevant Sections:**

**Key Concepts:**

**Common Terminology:**
- "Provoked"
- "Loss of self-control"

- "Ordinary person"

**Case Law Keywords:**

- *R v Van Den Hoek* [1986]: Ordinary person test

#### Mistake of Fact
**Definition:** Honest and reasonable belief in facts which, if true, would make conduct innocent.

**Elements:**
1. Honest belief in facts
2. Belief reasonable in circumstances

**Relevant Sections:**

#### Intoxication
**Definition:** Impairment by alcohol or drugs affecting capacity to form intent or understand conduct.

### Sentencing Principles

#### Purposes of Sentencing
**Key Purposes:**
1. **Just punishment (retribution):** Punishment proportionate to offending
2. **Deterrence:**
   - General deterrence (deter others)
   - Specific deterrence (deter offender)
3. **Rehabilitation:** Reform and reintegrate offender
4. **Protection of community (incapacitation)**
5. **Denunciation:** Express community condemnation
6. **Reparation:** Compensation to victims

**Relevant Sections:**
- Crimes Act 1914 (Cth) s 16A(2): Matters for consideration
- Crimes (Sentencing Procedure) Act 1999 (NSW) s 3A: Purposes
- Sentencing Act 1991 (Vic) s 5: Purposes

- Penalties and Sentences Act 1992 (Qld) s 9: Sentencing principles
- Sentencing Act 1995 (WA) s 6: Purposes
- Sentencing Act 1997 (Tas) s 3: Purposes

#### Aggravating Factors
**Common Aggravating Factors:**
- Seriousness of offence
- Use of weapon
- Victim vulnerability
- Breach of trust
- Offence committed on bail
- Premeditation/planning
- Multiple victims
- Group offending
- Offence motivated by prejudice (hate crime)
- Significant harm caused
- Abuse of position of authority
- Previous convictions (particularly similar)
- Lack of remorse

**Relevant Sections:**
- Crimes Act 1914 (Cth) s 16A(2)(a)-(n)
- Crimes (Sentencing Procedure) Act 1999 (NSW) s 21A(2)
- Sentencing Act 1991 (Vic) s 5(2)

#### Mitigating Factors
**Common Mitigating Factors:**
- Early guilty plea (discount 10-25%)
- Remorse and contrition
- Cooperation with authorities
- Assistance to prosecution
- Good character/no prior convictions
- Youth or age of offender
- Mental impairment or illness
- Prospects of rehabilitation
- Delay in prosecution
- Hardship to offender or dependants
- Provocation (not amounting to defence)
- Diminished responsibility
- Voluntary disclosure
- Steps toward reparation

**Relevant Sections:**
- Crimes Act 1914 (Cth) s 16A(2)(a)-(n)
- Crimes Act 1914 (Cth) s 16BA: Guilty plea discount
- Crimes (Sentencing Procedure) Act 1999 (NSW) s 21A(3)
- Crimes (Sentencing Procedure) Act 1999 (NSW) s 22: Guilty plea discount
- Sentencing Act 1991 (Vic) s 5(2)

#### Sentencing Options

**Commonwealth:**
1. Dismissal or discharge (s 19B(1)(c))
2. Good behaviour bond (s 20(1)(a))
3. Fine (s 4B)
4. Community service (s 20AB)
5. Imprisonment (s 17A)
6. Intensive correction order
7. Home detention
8. Suspended sentence (abolished in some jurisdictions)

**State Variations:**
- Imprisonment (full-time custody)
- Intensive correction order (ICO)
- Community correction order (CCO)
- Conditional release order (CRO)
- Home detention
- Fine
- Good behaviour bond/recognizance
- Section 10 dismissal/conditional release (NSW)
- Adjourned undertaking (SA)
- Youth justice order (juveniles)

#### Mandatory Sentencing
**Application:**
- Limited Commonwealth offences
- Some State offences (particularly NT, WA)
- Controversial policy area

**Examples:**
- Murder (life imprisonment—most jurisdictions)
- Some serious sexual offences
- Serious drug trafficking (some jurisdictions)
- Repeat drink driving (some jurisdictions)
- Home burglary (NT—limited periods)

#### Standard/Guideline Sentences
**NSW:** Standard non-parole periods (Division 1A, Part 4)
- Guide for mid-range objective seriousness
- Applies to serious offences
- Mandatory consideration

**Other Jurisdictions:**
- Victoria: Baseline sentences (abolished 2017)
- Queensland: No standard sentences
- Most use guideline judgments from appeal courts

## 4. Court Hierarchy for Criminal Matters

### Magistrates Court (Local Court NSW)
**Jurisdiction:**
- Summary offences
- Committal hearings for indictable offences
- Indictable offences dealt with summarily (with consent)
- Bail applications
- Sentencing for summary matters

**Sentencing Powers:**
- Maximum 2 years imprisonment (single offence—most jurisdictions)
- Maximum 5 years imprisonment (multiple offences—some jurisdictions)
- Fines
- Community-based orders

**Procedures:**
- No jury
- Less formal than higher courts
- Most criminal matters heard here
- Committal hearings to determine if sufficient evidence for trial

**Alternative Names:**
- Local Court (NSW)
- Magistrates' Court (Vic, Qld, WA, SA, Tas, NT, ACT)

### Children's Court
**Jurisdiction:**
- Criminal matters involving children/young persons
- Age ranges vary by jurisdiction (typically under 18)
- Summary and indictable offences (except most serious)

**Key Features:**
- Closed court (protect identity)
- Focus on rehabilitation
- Specialist magistrates
- Youth justice principles
- Diversion programs

**Sentencing Options:**
- Youth justice orders
- Community-based sentences
- Detention (last resort)
- Restorative justice conferencing

### District/County Court
**Jurisdiction:**
- Most indictable offences
- Appeals from Magistrates Court
- Sentencing following committal

**Excluded Offences (reserved for Supreme Court):**
- Murder
- Attempted murder
- Manslaughter
- Treason
- Piracy
- Some serious sexual offences (varies by jurisdiction)

**Sentencing Powers:**
- No limitation on imprisonment terms
- All sentencing options available

**Trial Process:**
- Judge and jury
- Formal criminal trial procedure
- Prosecution and defence counsel
- Rules of evidence strictly applied

**Alternative Names:**
- District Court (NSW, Qld, SA, WA)
- County Court (Vic)
- N/A (ACT, Tas, NT—Supreme Court exercises this jurisdiction)

### Supreme Court
**Jurisdiction:**
- Most serious indictable offences
- Murder and manslaughter
- Treason
- Serious sexual offences (some jurisdictions)
- Appeals from District/County Court
- Applications for judicial review

**Trial Division:**
- Judge and jury (indictable trials)
- Judge alone (if elected and approved)
- Unlimited sentencing powers

**Court of Criminal Appeal:**
- Appeals against conviction
- Appeals against sentence
- Prosecution appeals (limited)
- Question of law appeals
- Panel of judges (usually 3)

**Key Features:**
- Superior court of record
- Unlimited jurisdiction
- Sets precedents for lower courts
- Supervisory jurisdiction over inferior courts

### High Court of Australia
**Jurisdiction:**
- Final court of appeal
- Constitutional matters
- Appeals from State Supreme Courts (with special leave)
- Matters of public importance
- Commonwealth offences appeals

**Criminal Matters:**
- Special leave required (most cases)
- Questions of law of general importance
- Constitutional challenges
- Conflicting State appeal decisions
- Miscarriage of justice

**Key Features:**
- 7 justices (full bench) or 5 justices
- Binds all Australian courts
- Final interpreter of Constitution
- Limited criminal jurisdiction

### Court of Appeal/Criminal Appeal
**Jurisdiction:**
- Appeals from District/County Court trials
- Appeals from Supreme Court trials
- Sentence appeals
- Crown appeals (limited)
- Questions of law reserved

**Grounds of Appeal:**
1. **Conviction Appeals:**
   - Error of law
   - Unreasonable verdict
   - Miscarriage of justice
   - Fresh evidence
   - Jury directions inadequate

2. **Sentence Appeals:**
   - Manifestly excessive (defence)
   - Manifestly inadequate (Crown)
   - Error in principle
   - New facts emerged

**Powers:**
- Dismiss appeal
- Allow appeal and quash conviction
- Order new trial
- Substitute verdict
- Vary sentence
- Dismiss and increase sentence (Crown appeal)

**Alternative Names:**
- Court of Criminal Appeal (NSW, Qld, SA, Tas, NT)
- Court of Appeal, Criminal Division (Vic, WA, ACT)
- High Court (appeals in Tas, NT—limited)

## 5. Citation Patterns

### Crown Prosecutions
**Format:** R v [Defendant Surname]

- R v Li [2016] VSCA 145

- Regina (Queen)
- Rex (King)
- The Crown
- The State

**Variations:**
- Rex v [Defendant] (historical, during King's reign)
- Regina v [Defendant] (during Queen's reign)
- The King v [Defendant] (current, Charles III)

### DPP Prosecutions
**Format:** DPP v [Defendant Surname] or [Director of Public Prosecutions] v [Defendant]

**Examples:**
- DPP v Smith [2020] VSCA 89

### Appeals by Defendant
**Format:** [Defendant Surname] v The Queen or [Defendant] v The King

**Examples:**
- Smith v The Queen [2021] HCA 32
- Anderson v The Queen (2018) 265 CLR 323
- Lee v The Queen [2014] NSWCCA 234
- Jones v The King [2024] HCA 5

**Notes:**
- Used when defendant appeals conviction or sentence
- "The Queen" or "The King" represents the Crown/State
- Change reflects current monarch

### Appeals by Crown
**Format:** The Queen v [Defendant Surname] or R v [Defendant]; Crown v [Defendant]

**Examples:**
- The Queen v Hili (2010) 242 CLR 520
- R v Smith [2019] NSWCCA 145 (Crown appeal)
- Regina v Anderson [2018] VSCA 89 (Crown sentence appeal)

**Crown Appeal Types:**
1. **Against acquittal (question of law only):**
   - Very limited grounds
   - Cannot reverse acquittal
   - For precedent clarification

2. **Against sentence (manifestly inadequate):**
   - Crown appeals sentence as too lenient
   - Can increase sentence
   - "Guideline" or "standard of sentencing" cases

## 9. Interstate Variations Summary

### Homicide
- **QLD, WA, TAS:** Retain code-based definitions
- **NSW, VIC, SA, ACT:** Common law with statutory modifications
- **Provocation:** Abolished (Vic, WA, Tas), retained in modified form (NSW, Qld, SA)
- **Defensive homicide:** Introduced and abolished in Victoria (2005-2014)

### Sexual Offences
- Significant variations in:
  - Consent definitions
  - Age of consent (generally 16, but variations)
  - Affirmative consent requirements (Tas, Vic reforms)
  - Defences available

### Property Offences
- **Code States (Qld, WA, Tas):** Comprehensive code definitions
- **Common Law States (NSW, Vic, SA):** Mix of common law and statute
- Theft/larceny terminology differences

### Sentencing
- **Suspended sentences:** Abolished in some jurisdictions (Vic, Qld for serious offences)
- **Standard/guideline sentences:** NSW (standard non-parole periods), Vic (baseline sentences abolished)
- **Maximum penalties:** Significant variations between jurisdictions

### Defences
- **Self-defence:** Different statutory formulations
- **Mental impairment:** Separate legislation in some jurisdictions (Vic)
- **Provocation:** Different tests and availability

2. **Offence types:** Murder, assault, sexual offences, theft, drug offences
3. **Court level:** Magistrates, District, Supreme, Court of Criminal Appeal
4. **Citation pattern:** R v [Name], DPP v [Name], [Name] v The Queen
5. **Procedural stage:** Committal, trial, appeal, sentencing

### Secondary Indicators
6. **Defences raised:** Self-defence, duress, mental impairment, provocation
7. **Sentencing matters:** Aggravation, mitigation, penalty type, discount
8. **Evidentiary issues:** Admissibility, tendency, hearsay

- Civil claims for assault (tort)

- Coronial inquests (unless related to criminal proceedings)

- Domestic violence charges

- Deportation following conviction

- → Classify based on primary issue in case

## 11. Commonwealth vs State Criminal Law

### Constitutional Division
- Commonwealth: Specific heads of power (s 51 Constitution)
- States: Residual/plenary power over criminal law
- Concurrent operation possible
- Commonwealth law prevails if inconsistent (s 109)

### Commonwealth Criminal Jurisdiction
**Key Heads of Power:**
- Trade and commerce (s 51(i))
- Taxation (s 51(ii))
- Postal, telegraphic, telephonic services (s 51(v))
- Defence (s 51(vi))
- Immigration (s 51(xxvii))
- External affairs (s 51(xxix))
- Territories (s 122)

**Key Commonwealth Offences:**
- Terrorism and national security
- People smuggling and trafficking
- Importation offences (drugs, weapons)
- Serious financial crime (taxation, corporate)
- Cybercrime (telecommunications)
- Child sex offences overseas
- Crimes against humanity

### State Criminal Jurisdiction
**General Criminal Law:**
- Murder, manslaughter
- Assault, sexual offences
- Theft, robbery, burglary
- Fraud (non-Commonwealth)
- Drug offences (domestic supply)

- Summary offences

### Concurrent Jurisdiction
Some offences may breach both Commonwealth and State law:
- Fraud involving Commonwealth agencies
- Drug importation and supply
- Cybercrime
- Child exploitation

**Prosecution Choice:**
- Generally State prosecutions for State offences
- Commonwealth DPP for Commonwealth offences
- Memoranda of Understanding govern jurisdictional choices
- Avoid double jeopardy
