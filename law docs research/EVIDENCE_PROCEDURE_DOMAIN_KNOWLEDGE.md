# Evidence & Procedural Law Domain Knowledge

## Overview
This document provides comprehensive domain knowledge for Evidence and Procedural Law in Australian jurisdictions. It covers both uniform evidence legislation and non-uniform state provisions, as well as procedural rules governing civil and criminal proceedings.

## 3. Key Legal Concepts & Keywords

### Admissibility Concepts
- **Relevance** - rational effect on probability of fact in issue
- **Probative value** - strength of evidence in proving fact
- **Prejudicial effect** - risk of unfair prejudice or misuse
- **Weight** - value to be given to admitted evidence
- **Exclusionary rules** - rules preventing admission
- **Voir dire** - trial within a trial to determine admissibility
- **Facts in issue** - ultimate facts to be proved
- **Circumstantial evidence** - indirect evidence requiring inference
- **Direct evidence** - evidence directly proving fact

### Hearsay Concepts
- **Representation** - assertion or statement
- **Previous representation** - out-of-court statement
- **Intended assertion** - purpose of statement
- **First-hand hearsay** - direct knowledge of witness
- **Second-hand hearsay** - knowledge from another source
- **Business records** - documents created in course of business
- **Contemporaneous** - made at time of event
- **Personal knowledge** - direct observation or experience

### Opinion Evidence Concepts
- **Specialised knowledge** - expertise based on training/experience
- **Basis rule** - facts underlying opinion must be proved
- **Ultimate issue rule** - opinion on fact to be decided (abolished in UEA)
- **Unchallengeable opinion** - expert invading province of jury
- **Concurrent evidence** - experts giving evidence together
- **Expert shopping** - seeking favourable expert opinions

### Privilege Concepts
- **Client legal privilege** - confidential lawyer-client communications
- **Dominant purpose test** - main purpose of communication
- **Loss of privilege** - waiver express or implied
- **Inconsistency waiver** - deploying privileged material
- **Common interest privilege** - shared privilege among parties
- **Self-incrimination** - privilege against compulsory answers
- **Public interest immunity** - Crown privilege for state secrets
- **Without prejudice** - settlement negotiation protection
- **Legal professional privilege** - common law equivalent

### Procedural Concepts
- **Res judicata** - matter already decided (cause of action estoppel)
- **Issue estoppel** - particular issue already determined
- **Anshun estoppel** - failure to raise matter in prior proceeding
- **Abuse of process** - misuse of court procedures
- **Summary judgment** - determination without trial
- **Strike out** - dismissal of pleading or claim
- **Pleadings** - statements of claim, defence, reply
- **Particulars** - details of pleaded allegations
- **Joinder** - joining parties or causes of action
- **Consolidation** - combining separate proceedings
- **Vexatious litigant** - person habitually instituting unmeritorious proceedings

### Discovery & Disclosure Concepts
- **Discovery** - disclosure of documents in party's possession/control
- **Inspection** - right to examine discovered documents
- **Privilege log** - list of privileged documents claimed
- **Oppressive** - unduly burdensome or harassing
- **Fishing expedition** - seeking documents without legitimate purpose
- **Legitimate forensic purpose** - proper use in proceedings
- **Implied undertaking** - obligation to use documents only for proceedings
- **Pre-action discovery** - discovery before commencing proceedings
- **Norwich Pharmacal order** - discovery from third party to identify wrongdoer

### Evidence Rules & Principles
- **Corroboration** - independent evidence supporting testimony
- **Tendency evidence** - evidence of character or pattern of conduct
- **Coincidence evidence** - improbability of similar events occurring
- **Similar fact evidence** - evidence of other acts
- **Propensity evidence** - evidence of tendency to act in particular way
- **Credit** - credibility and reliability of witness
- **Impeachment** - challenging credibility of witness
- **Cross-examination** - questioning by opposing party
- **Leading question** - question suggesting answer
- **Hostile witness** - witness antagonistic to calling party
- **Refresh memory** - using document to aid recollection
- **Past recollection recorded** - hearsay exception for recorded memory

### Burden & Proof Concepts
- **Beyond reasonable doubt** - criminal standard of proof
- **Balance of probabilities** - civil standard of proof
- **Briginshaw standard** - higher civil standard for serious matters
- **Legal burden** - obligation to prove fact
- **Evidential burden** - obligation to adduce evidence
- **Prima facie case** - case sufficient to require answer
- **No case to answer** - insufficient evidence to continue
- **Judicial notice** - facts court accepts without proof
- **Presumption** - inference of fact until rebutted
- **Reverse onus** - burden on defendant to disprove

### Trial & Hearing Concepts
- **Opening address** - outline of case at trial commencement
- **Closing submissions** - final arguments after evidence
- **Examination in chief** - questioning own witness
- **Cross-examination** - questioning opponent's witness
- **Re-examination** - further questions after cross-examination
- **Objection** - challenge to admissibility of evidence
- **Ruling** - judicial decision on procedural matter
- **Direction** - judge's instruction to jury
- **Verdict** - jury's decision on guilt/liability
- **Judgment** - court's formal decision and reasons

## 4. Rules of Evidence (Detailed)

### 4.1 Hearsay Rule & Exceptions

#### The Hearsay Rule (s 59 UEA)
Evidence of a previous representation made by a person is not admissible to prove the existence of a fact that it can reasonably be supposed that the person intended to assert by the representation.

#### First-Hand Hearsay Exceptions

**s 63 - Civil proceedings - maker available**
- Maker available to be called
- Notice requirements
- Reasonable practicability considerations

**s 64 - Civil proceedings - maker not available**
- Maker deceased, unfit, outside Australia
- Cannot be found or identified
- Unavailable for other reasons

**s 65 - Criminal proceedings - maker available**
- Accused consent required (if against accused)
- Maker available for cross-examination
- Fresh in memory requirement

**s 66 - Criminal proceedings - maker not available**
- Same unavailability grounds as s 64
- Maker must have had personal knowledge
- More stringent reliability requirements

**s 66A - Exception - contemporaneous statements about health**
- Representations about person's health, feelings, sensations, intention, knowledge, state of mind
- Made when asserted fact fresh in memory

#### Business Records Exception (s 69 UEA)
- Document part of business records
- Representation made by person with personal knowledge
- Made or received in course of business
- "Business" broadly defined
- Electronic records included

#### Other Hearsay Exceptions

**s 70 - Tags and labels**
- Attached to or written on objects
- Identifying or describing objects

**s 71 - Electronic communications**
- Origin, destination, or time of electronic communication
- Presumptions about electronic communications

**s 72 - Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander traditional laws and customs**
- Evidence of traditional laws and customs
- From person with specialised knowledge

**s 73 - Reputation as to relationships and age**
- Public or general reputation
- Family reputation

**s 74 - Reputation of public or general rights**
- Public rights or general rights

**s 75 - Interlocutory proceedings**
- Generally admissible in interlocutory proceedings

### 4.2 Opinion Rule & Exceptions

#### The Opinion Rule (s 76 UEA)
Evidence of an opinion is not admissible to prove the existence of a fact about the existence of which the opinion was expressed.

#### Lay Opinion Exception (s 78 UEA)
Admissible if:
- Opinion based on what person saw, heard or otherwise perceived
- Opinion necessary to obtain adequate account of perception
- Common examples:
  - Age, speed, identity
  - Emotional state
  - Intoxication
  - Physical condition

#### Expert Opinion Exception (s 79 UEA)
Admissible if:
- Person has specialised knowledge (training, study, experience)
- Opinion wholly or substantially based on that knowledge
- Common fields:
  - Medical opinions
  - Forensic evidence
  - Engineering assessments
  - Accounting/financial opinions
  - Handwriting analysis
  - DNA evidence
  - Psychological/psychiatric evidence

#### Common Law Expert Evidence Principles
- Makita principles (expert report requirements)
- Expert independence and impartiality
- Concurrent evidence procedures
- Expert code of conduct
- Court's gatekeeper role
- Reliability of novel techniques

### 4.3 Credibility Rules

#### Credibility Evidence (s 101A UEA)
Evidence relevant only to credibility of witness

#### Credibility Rule (s 102 UEA)
Credibility evidence about witness not admissible

#### Exceptions to Credibility Rule

**s 103 - Cross-examination as to credibility**
- Substantial probative value test
- Relevant to credibility
- Prior inconsistent statements

**s 104 - Re-establishing credibility**
- Response to attack on credibility in cross-examination

**s 106 - Exception: rebutting denials by other evidence**
- After witness denies matter in cross-examination
- Substantial probative value required
- Court's leave needed

**s 108A - Exception: admissions**
- Admissions relevant to credibility admissible

**s 108B - Exception: character of accused**
- Accused may lead evidence of good character

**s 108C - Exception: specialised knowledge**
- Expert evidence about credibility (rare)

#### Prior Inconsistent Statements
- May be used to impeach credibility
- Procedural requirements (confrontation)
- May prove truth of facts under s 60 UEA

#### Bias, Corruption or Concoction
- Evidence showing motive to lie
- Evidence of fabrication
- Evidence of influence on testimony

### 4.4 Character Evidence

#### Character of Accused (Criminal Proceedings)

**s 110 - Evidence about character of accused**
- Prosecution cannot lead evidence of bad character
- Unless accused puts character in issue
- Or evidence relevant to non-character purpose

**Loss of Shield**
- Accused attacks prosecution witness
- Accused gives evidence of good character
- Nature of defence involves imputations

#### Tendency Evidence (s 97 UEA)
Evidence of character, reputation or conduct to prove:
- Tendency to act in particular way
- Tendency to have particular state of mind
Requirements:
- Court must give notice
- Significant probative value
- Cannot use coincidence reasoning

#### Coincidence Evidence (s 98 UEA)
Evidence of similar events to prove:
- Improbability of events occurring coincidentally
Requirements:
- Court must give notice
- Significant probative value
- Cannot use tendency reasoning

**Relationship Between Tendency and Coincidence**
- Mutually exclusive pathways
- Different reasoning processes
- IMM v The Queen (2016) guidance
- Hughes v The Queen (2017) clarification

#### Character in Civil Proceedings
- Generally not relevant unless in issue
- Defamation proceedings
- Malicious prosecution
- Some tort claims

## 10. Drafting & Practice Tips

### Evidence Objections
- State the specific ground clearly (e.g., "Objection, hearsay under s 59")
- Reference specific statutory provision or rule
- Be prepared to argue probative value vs prejudice
- Request voir dire if necessary

### Notice Requirements
- Tendency/coincidence: reasonable notice (s 97-98)
- Expert evidence: comply with practice notes
- Hearsay in civil proceedings: reasonable notice (s 67)

### Expert Evidence
- Comply with Makita requirements
- Include qualifications and expertise
- State factual assumptions clearly
- Express opinions based on specialised knowledge
- Acknowledge limitations

### Discovery
- Commence early in proceedings
- Identify categories clearly
- Consider proportionality
- Claim privilege with specificity
- Maintain privilege log

### Privilege Claims
- Assert clearly and promptly
- Provide evidentiary basis
- Avoid waiver through disclosure
- Consider common interest arrangements
- Be cautious of implied waiver
